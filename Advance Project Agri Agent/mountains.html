mountains and forest soil

Mountain ecosystems are unique due to their varied climates and altitudes. As you go higher, the temperature drops and the environment changes, creating different habitats. These ecosystems are home to a diverse range of plants and animals, many of which are specially adapted to the harsh conditions Forest soils in mountainous regions develop more slowly compared to other areas.

About Nutrition In Percentage
Nitrogen levels can be lower in high-altitude soils due to reduced organic matter decomposition and slower biological activity. Nitrogen (N) 0.05% to 0.15%
Phosphorus availability can be limited in mountain soils, particularly if the soil is acidic or if there is high soil erosion. Phosphorus (P) 5-15%
Potassium levels might be somewhat higher but still vary based on parent material and weathering processes. Potassium (K): 0.05% to 0.2%
Magnesium levels are generally lower compared to other nutrients but can vary based on soil parent material and local conditions. Magnesium (Mg) 0.02% to 0.1%
Sulfur levels can be quite low, though they may increase in areas with high organic matter or pollution. Sulfur (S) 0.01% to 0.03%
