Saline and Alkaline Soil

Alkali, or Alkaline, soils are clay soils with high pH (greater than 8.5), a poor soil structure and a low infiltration capacity. Often they have a hard calcareous layer at 0.5 to 1 metre depth. Alkali soils owe their unfavorable physico-chemical properties mainly to the dominating presence of sodium carbonate, which causes the soil to swell[1] and difficult to clarify/settle. They derive their name from the alkali metal group of elements, to which sodium belongs, and which can induce basicity. Sometimes these soils are also referred to as alkaline sodic soils. Alkaline soils are basic, but not all basic soils are alkaline.

About Crop Nutrition

About Nutrition In Percentage
Sodium is present in high concentrations in alkali soils, often comprising more of the cation exchange capacity (CEC) in these soils. Sodium (Na): 5-15%
Calcium helps in flocculating soil particles and improving soil structure, which is often compromised in alkali soils. Calcium (Ca): less than 0.6.
Magnesium levels may also be lower compared to sodium. This affects soil structure and can exacerbate soil dispersion Magnesium (Mg)
Potassium levels can be low due to its interaction with sodium. Adequate potassium is crucial for plant water regulation and enzyme function. Potassium (K):
Phosphorus availability is often reduced in alkali soils because high pH levels can cause phosphorus to precipitate as insoluble compounds. Phosphorus (P) 1-2%
Essential micronutrients like iron (Fe), manganese (Mn), zinc (Zn), and copper (Cu) can become less available in high pH conditions. Micronutrients
